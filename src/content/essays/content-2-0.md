Most companies treat content as a marketing function — fragmented across departments, measured by vanity metrics, managed through agencies disconnected from the business, or left to a junior hire with no system around them. The result is significant spend with unmeasurable return, at exactly the moment content has the highest leverage.

This is an essay about why that model is broken, what replaces it, and why the replacement only became possible in the last twelve months.

---

## The age-old content problem

There is a gap in how early-stage and growth-stage companies handle content. Two obvious solutions exist, and neither works. Agencies produce content but are disconnected from business context, expensive, and leave when the contract ends. A full-time senior content hire costs €80,000–120,000 a year — unaffordable at Series A or earlier. A junior hire is affordable but lacks the capability to build a system.

The gap between agency execution and an unaffordable senior hire is where most early-stage companies get stuck. Content happens, but it never becomes a system. And without a system, content cannot compound.

## Content 1.0 — the dominant model

What I call Content 1.0 is the model most companies still run: organised by channel or format, fragmented across departments, measured by vanity metrics, disconnected from business objectives. The sales deck doesn't know what the job listing says. The investor update doesn't know what the blog published last week. Each piece of content is an island.

> Much of today's AI deployment falls into this category — incremental improvement rather than full transformation. To unlock real value, companies need to look beyond optimising individual tasks and start reimagining entire workflows.
>
> — Andrew Ng

Most AI adoption in content today is exactly this: individual tools bolted onto Content 1.0 workflows. A better copywriting tool. A faster image generator. The workflow itself remains unchanged.

## What changed in 2026

The maturation of agentic AI in early 2026 — specifically the emergence of capable multi-agent coordination, reliable tool-calling, and autonomous deployment and iteration capabilities — makes something new possible. Not better tools within the old workflow, but an entirely different workflow.

For the first time, it is technically feasible to build a system where content is produced, deployed, measured, and improved in a continuous loop — with minimal human intervention. Not a tool. An operating system.

## Content as infrastructure

Content 2.0 reframes content as organisational infrastructure — not a marketing function but a company-wide operating system. In the same way a financial system runs across every department and serves every business function, a content system should be designed once, maintained centrally, and deployed everywhere.

This is not a metaphor. It is an architectural claim. A company's content operation can be built as a literal system — with defined inputs, a production engine, deployment logic, measurement feedback, and self-improvement loops — that serves every function in the business: sales, talent, investors, community, customer success, internal culture.

## The content operating system

A content operating system has two layers. The first is the demand side: every business function has jobs to be done that require content. These are organised into clusters — groupings of related content needs that share an audience, a purpose, and a success metric.

The second layer is the supply side: a content engine that receives briefs from those clusters and produces content in response. The engine knows the company — its voice, positioning, competitive landscape, audience intelligence, and content history. It generates content and simultaneously generates the KPIs for that content. It deploys across channels, analyses performance, and iterates. The system learns.

Critically, the engine knows all content simultaneously and maintains coherence across all of it. A job listing knows what the sales deck says. When the sales cluster learns something, the talent cluster benefits immediately. Cross-functional learnings are adopted instantaneously. This is a flywheel that compounds over time.

## What this means for companies

Installing a content operating system makes content part of the fabric of a company. Content is not produced extraneously. Content becomes an extension of the core business functions themselves.

The concept of a content operating system belongs to the world. I am publishing it because I believe every company will eventually run one. The question is not whether, but when — and how well.
